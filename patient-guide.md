# Understanding Gluten Problems in Babies: A Guide for Parents

**Prepared for patients and caregivers**
**Date:** 2026-05-26

---

## What Is It?

Some babies and young children have trouble with gluten. Gluten is a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye — ingredients in many common foods like bread, pasta, and crackers.

The most common gluten-related problem is called **celiac disease**. In celiac disease, the body's immune system reacts to gluten as if it were a threat. This reaction damages the lining of the small intestine, making it harder for the body to absorb nutrients from food.

Celiac disease is not a food allergy in the traditional sense — it is an autoimmune condition. It is also hereditary. If a parent or sibling has celiac disease, a baby's chance of developing it is about 1 in 10, compared to about 1 in 100 in the general population.

A different condition called **gluten sensitivity** (sometimes called gluten intolerance) can also cause discomfort after eating gluten. Unlike celiac disease, it does not damage the intestine. It is less well understood and harder to diagnose.

---

## How Does It Work?

When a baby with celiac disease eats gluten, the immune system attacks the lining of the small intestine. Over time, this damages the tiny finger-like projections (called villi) that help absorb nutrients from food.

This can lead to problems absorbing important nutrients like iron, calcium, and vitamins. In babies, this often shows up as poor weight gain or slower-than-expected growth.

Celiac disease usually becomes noticeable after a baby starts eating solid foods that contain gluten — typically around 6 to 9 months of age.

---

## What Does the Research Show?

Research involving thousands of babies and children has taught doctors several important things:

- **When gluten is introduced matters — but only within a range.** Doctors recommend introducing gluten-containing foods between 4 and 12 months of age. Waiting until after 12 months does not appear to protect against celiac disease and may slightly increase the risk.
- **Breastfeeding does not prevent celiac disease.** Studies have found that whether a baby is breastfed or not does not change whether they will develop celiac disease. Breastfeeding is still important for many other reasons, however.
- **Large amounts of gluten early in life may increase risk** in children who are genetically at risk. Introducing gluten gradually and in reasonable portions during the first year is generally recommended.
- **The only treatment for celiac disease is a strict gluten-free diet — for life.** Even tiny amounts of gluten can trigger a reaction in a child with confirmed celiac disease. Most babies who start a gluten-free diet improve noticeably within just two weeks.
- **A gluten-free diet needs careful planning.** Cutting out gluten can lead to gaps in important nutrients. Working with a dietitian helps ensure a child gets everything they need to grow and thrive.

---

## What Should I Ask My Doctor?

Here are questions to bring to your child's next appointment:

1. **My baby has been having diarrhea, bloating, or isn't gaining weight well. Could gluten be a factor?**
2. **Someone in our family has celiac disease. Should my baby be tested? When and how?**
3. **What is the right time to introduce gluten-containing foods for my baby?**
4. **If my child needs testing for celiac disease, do they need to keep eating gluten before the test?**
5. **If my child is diagnosed with celiac disease, who can help us with a safe, nutritionally complete gluten-free diet?**
6. **How will we know if the gluten-free diet is working? What do we watch for?**
7. **Does my child need genetic testing to see if they are at higher risk?**
