# Executive Summary: Gluten Allergy/Intolerance in Infants

**Audience:** Clinician
**Date:** 2026-05-26

---

## Key Findings

Gluten-related disorders presenting in infancy encompass two distinct clinical entities: celiac disease (CD), an autoimmune enteropathy, and non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS), a non-immune-mediated condition. Celiac disease remains substantially underdiagnosed in pediatric populations due to its highly variable clinical phenotype, which frequently departs from the classic malabsorption presentation [Lemale2022].

**Clinical presentation in infants and toddlers** includes chronic diarrhea, abdominal bloating and distension, constipation, vomiting, fussiness, and — critically — failure to thrive, defined as inadequate weight gain or growth for age [NIDDK2024; NLM2024; ClevelandClinic2025]. Symptoms typically become clinically apparent after gluten-containing complementary foods are introduced, most often between 6 and 12 months of age [Schaer2024]. Extraintestinal features such as fatigue, irritability, bone or joint pain, and dermatitis herpetiformis may also occur [NLM2024].

**Non-celiac gluten sensitivity** in children does not carry the same autoimmune or structural consequences. Intestinal architecture is preserved, and no validated serological marker exists. Diagnosis relies on a systematic elimination and rechallenge protocol [Pasquariello2014].

**Regarding gluten introduction timing**, the weight of evidence from multiple large randomized controlled trials has overturned earlier assumptions. The PreventCD trial (n=944, HLA-DQ2/DQ8-positive infants) found no reduction in celiac disease incidence by age 3 with early small-dose gluten introduction at 16–24 weeks compared to placebo [Vriezinga2014]. A systematic review and meta-analysis identified that late introduction (after 6 months) increased CD risk by approximately 25% compared to the 4–6 month window, but this finding reflects association rather than protective mechanism [PintoSanchez2016]. The 2024 ESPGHAN update — the most current major guideline — concluded definitively that timing of gluten introduction between 4 and 12 completed months does not affect cumulative CD incidence [Szajewska2024].

**Breastfeeding** confers no demonstrated protection against celiac disease development, regardless of exclusivity or duration, as confirmed by both RCT data and the 2024 ESPGHAN position paper [Szajewska2024; Lopez2015]. One observational study noted that extended breastfeeding may delay the age of clinical manifestation, without affecting ultimate disease development [Radlovic2010].

**Gluten quantity** in early childhood may be a relevant modifiable variable. Higher cumulative gluten intake during weaning and the second and third years of life has been associated with increased CD risk in at-risk cohorts, though no universal gluten restriction is recommended for the general infant population [AndrenAronsson2023; Szajewska2024].

---

## Clinical Implications

1. **Threshold for testing should be low** in infants presenting with failure to thrive, chronic diarrhea, or unexplained growth deceleration after gluten introduction — even in the absence of classic malabsorption symptoms [Lemale2022; NIDDK2024].

2. **First-line serology** consists of total serum IgA and IgA anti-tissue transglutaminase (TTG-IgA). In children under 2 years, TTG-IgA and endomysial antibody (EMA-IgA) may be less accurate, warranting caution in interpretation [RubioTapia2023; NICE2015].

3. **A nonbiopsy diagnostic pathway** is supported by the ACG (2023) for selected children when TTG-IgA exceeds 10 times the upper limit of normal and EMA is positive [RubioTapia2023]. Endoscopic biopsy may still be required when serological criteria are not met [Lemale2022].

4. **Do not initiate serological testing before gluten has been introduced** into the infant's diet, as pre-gluten serology is diagnostically unreliable [NICE2015]. Similarly, instruct caregivers to maintain a gluten-containing diet throughout the diagnostic workup to avoid false-negative results [Klemm2022].

5. **Gluten introduction timing** need not be manipulated in the general population or even in first-degree relatives of CD patients. Introduction at any point between 4 and 12 completed months is appropriate [Szajewska2024; Szajewska2016].

6. **Definitive treatment** remains strict, lifelong adherence to a gluten-free diet (GFD). Infants with confirmed CD typically show clinical improvement within two weeks of GFD initiation [Schaer2024; RubioTapia2023].

7. **Family history** significantly elevates risk: first-degree relatives carry a 1-in-10 risk versus a population baseline of approximately 1-in-100 [Schaer2024]. HLA typing (HLA-DQ2/DQ8) can stratify risk and guide surveillance decisions [Lopez2015].

## Evidence Quality

The evidence base for this topic is moderately strong for diagnostic and management recommendations, and mixed for prevention strategies.

- **Diagnosis and management** are supported by two major society guidelines (ACG 2023, ESPGHAN 2022/2024) and NICE NG20, all grounded in systematic reviews and expert consensus. These represent the highest quality guidance currently available for pediatric practice.
- **Gluten introduction timing** is addressed by two landmark RCTs (PreventCD [Vriezinga2014] and EAT Study [Hall2022]) and a systematic meta-analysis [PintoSanchez2016]. The RCT data are high-quality but yield conflicting directional signals — the EAT Study suggested a signal toward early introduction benefit, while PreventCD did not — and neither trial is adequately powered for definitive conclusions across diverse genetic backgrounds.
- **Breastfeeding as a protective factor** has been consistently refuted across RCT and observational data, though one older observational study suggested delayed manifestation with extended breastfeeding [Radlovic2010] — this should not be interpreted as altered disease incidence.
- **NCGS in infants** is supported only by observational and expert opinion-level evidence; no RCT data exist for this population.

---

## Gaps in Evidence

- **Optimal serological cut-offs in infants under 2 years** remain imprecise. Both ACG and NICE acknowledge reduced accuracy of TTG-IgA and EMA-IgA in this age group, yet no validated alternative algorithm exists.
- **Long-term outcomes** of infants diagnosed early and managed with strict GFD — including effects on growth trajectory, bone density, and neurodevelopment — require further prospective study.
- **NCGS in infancy** is poorly characterized. No validated diagnostic criteria, biomarkers, or natural history data exist for this age group.
- **Gluten dose thresholds** that modify CD risk in early childhood remain undefined. While high quantities appear associated with elevated risk [AndrenAronsson2023], no clinically actionable threshold has been established.
- **Preventive strategies** for genetically at-risk infants beyond current dietary recommendations are lacking. No pharmacological or immunological intervention has demonstrated efficacy in this population.
