# Clinical Deep Dive: Gluten Allergy/Intolerance in Infants

**Audience:** Clinician
**Date:** 2026-05-26

---

## 1. Background and Pathophysiology

Celiac disease (CD) is a chronic, immune-mediated systemic disorder characterized by small intestinal enteropathy triggered by dietary gluten in genetically predisposed individuals. Gluten — a collective term for storage proteins (gliadins and glutenins) found in wheat, barley, and rye — serves as the primary environmental trigger in susceptible hosts. The global prevalence of CD is approximately 1%, though many cases remain undiagnosed due to the condition's heterogeneous clinical expression [Lemale2022].

In pediatric populations, CD is particularly prone to underdiagnosis. Presentation in infants and young children spans a broad clinical spectrum: from the classic malabsorption syndrome (steatorrhea, abdominal distension, failure to thrive) to subtle or extraintestinal manifestations such as isolated iron-deficiency anemia, irritability, delayed puberty, or dermatitis herpetiformis. Symptoms typically emerge following the introduction of gluten-containing complementary foods, typically between 6 and 12 months of age [Schaer2024; ClevelandClinic2025].

Non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) — also referred to as gluten intolerance in lay literature — is a distinct entity. It is not an autoimmune disorder, does not involve specific IgE-mediated mechanisms, and does not cause structural damage to the small intestinal mucosa. Symptoms (abdominal pain, bloating, loose stool, fatigue) appear hours to days after gluten ingestion and resolve with elimination. No serological or histological marker is available for NCGS diagnosis in children [Pasquariello2014].

### Genetic Predisposition

CD is strongly HLA-restricted. Approximately 95% of patients carry HLA-DQ2 (encoded by HLA-DQA1*05 and HLA-DQB1*02), and most of the remainder carry HLA-DQ8 (encoded by HLA-DQA1*03 and HLA-DQB1*0302). Infants carrying HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 are at elevated risk for developing CD upon sustained gluten exposure [Lopez2015]. However, HLA positivity alone is insufficient for disease development; the majority of HLA-DQ2/DQ8 carriers never develop CD, indicating that additional genetic, environmental, and immunological factors contribute to disease expression [AndrenAronsson2023].

First-degree relatives of individuals with CD carry an approximately 10-fold elevated risk compared to the general population (roughly 10% vs. 1%) [Schaer2024], making family history an important clinical screening variable.

## 2. Mechanism of Action

In genetically susceptible individuals, ingested gluten peptides — particularly alpha-gliadin — resist complete proteolytic degradation in the gastrointestinal tract. These immunogenic peptides cross the intestinal epithelium and are deamidated by tissue transglutaminase 2 (tTG2), enhancing their affinity for HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8 molecules on antigen-presenting cells. Subsequent presentation to CD4+ T cells in the lamina propria triggers both innate and adaptive immune responses.

The resulting inflammatory cascade produces characteristic small intestinal histological changes: villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intraepithelial lymphocytosis (graded by the Marsh-Oberhuber classification). These structural changes impair nutrient absorption across the small intestinal surface, leading to malabsorption of fat, fat-soluble vitamins, iron, calcium, and other micronutrients. In infants, this manifests clinically as failure to thrive, growth faltering, and nutritional deficiencies [NIDDK2024; NLM2024].

Elevated serum IgA antibodies against tTG2 (TTG-IgA) and endomysium (EMA-IgA) are produced as part of the humoral immune response and serve as the primary serological markers for diagnosis. In young infants (under 2 years), antibody production may be quantitatively reduced, and total IgA deficiency — which occurs in approximately 2–3% of CD patients — can produce false-negative TTG-IgA results [RubioTapia2023].

NCGS, by contrast, does not involve tTG2 autoantibodies, mucosal inflammation, or villous atrophy. The pathophysiological mechanism remains incompletely understood but may involve innate immune activation rather than adaptive immune responses [Pasquariello2014].

## 3. Clinical Evidence

### Gluten Introduction Timing: RCT Evidence

Two landmark randomized controlled trials have directly tested whether gluten introduction timing modifies CD risk in at-risk infants:

**PreventCD Trial (Vriezinga et al., 2014, NEJM):** 944 infants with HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 genotype and a first-degree relative with CD were randomized to receive 100 mg of gluten daily or placebo beginning at 16–24 weeks of age. At age 3, celiac disease rates were similar between groups, demonstrating that early small-dose gluten introduction in high-risk infants did not reduce CD incidence. Early introduction did not delay or prevent autoimmunity development [Vriezinga2014].

**EAT Study (reported in Hall et al., 2022):** Early introduction of gluten at 4 months alongside breast milk resulted in zero confirmed CD cases by age 3 in the early introduction group versus 1.4% in the standard introduction group. This signal, while intriguing, requires cautious interpretation given population differences and the need for longer follow-up [Hall2022].

**Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (Pinto-Sánchez et al., 2016, J Pediatrics):** Analysis of 15 eligible studies found that late gluten introduction (after 6 months) increased CD risk by approximately 25% compared to the recommended 4–6 month window. Early introduction (before 4 months) did not significantly elevate risk. Breastfeeding at the time of gluten introduction showed no statistically significant protective effect [PintoSanchez2016].

### Gluten Quantity and Risk

Higher cumulative gluten intake during the weaning period and through the second and third years of life has been associated with increased CD incidence in at-risk cohorts, with CD incidence peaking in the second and third years of life [AndrenAronsson2023]. However, no RCT has specifically tested gluten quantity reduction as a prevention strategy, and no clinically actionable threshold has been defined.

### Breastfeeding: Evidence Summary

Multiple large prospective studies and RCT data have consistently failed to demonstrate a protective effect of breastfeeding — whether assessed by duration, exclusivity, or status at time of gluten introduction — against CD development [Szajewska2024; Lopez2015]. An older observational study by Radlovic et al. (2010) noted that extended breastfeeding significantly delayed clinical manifestation of CD in the first year of life, but did not reduce the ultimate incidence of disease [Radlovic2010]. This distinction — delay versus prevention — is clinically relevant when counseling families.

## 4. Current Guidelines

### ESPGHAN 2024 (Szajewska et al.)
The most current major guideline on early diet and CD risk concludes that:
- Gluten may be introduced at any time between completed 4 months and 12 months of age; timing does not affect cumulative CD incidence [Szajewska2024].
- Breastfeeding does not reduce CD risk.
- High quantities of gluten during weaning may correlate with increased risk in at-risk cohorts, though no population-level restriction is recommended.
- No universal preventive dietary strategy can be recommended for the general infant population [Szajewska2024].

### ESPGHAN 2016 (Szajewska et al.)
The preceding ESPGHAN position paper established that:
- Gluten may be introduced between 4 and 12 completed months.
- Large quantities should be avoided during the initial weeks after introduction and throughout infancy [Szajewska2016].

### ACG 2023 (Rubio-Tapia et al.)
- TTG-IgA is the preferred first-line serological test for CD detection in children under 2 years [RubioTapia2023].
- A nonbiopsy diagnostic pathway is supported in selected children when TTG-IgA exceeds 10 times the upper limit of normal and EMA-IgA is positive.
- Strict, lifelong GFD is the only currently accepted treatment [RubioTapia2023].
- Mass community screening is not recommended.

### NICE NG20 (2015)
- Do not offer serological testing before gluten has been introduced into the infant's diet [NICE2015].
- First-line tests: total IgA plus TTG-IgA.
- Positive serology requires referral to a pediatric gastroenterologist.
- Any diagnostic test is valid only during active gluten consumption [NICE2015].

### ESPGHAN Management and Follow-up 2022 (Mearin et al.)
- 37 evidence-based recommendations covering follow-up frequency, dietary adherence monitoring, growth surveillance, anemia management, and antibody level assessment [Mearin2022].
- Mucosal recovery (intestinal healing) should be a primary therapeutic goal.
- Special protocols required for children with concurrent type 1 diabetes, IgA deficiency, or diagnostically unclear cases.
- Quality of life evaluation and structured patient communication are integral to follow-up [Mearin2022].

## 5. Dosing and Administration (Gluten-Free Diet)

There is no established "dose" for the GFD in the conventional pharmacological sense. Rather, the critical parameter is the threshold of gluten exposure: even trace quantities can trigger antibody production and intestinal damage in confirmed CD [Klemm2022]. Clinicians should counsel caregivers on:

- Elimination of all wheat, barley, and rye from the infant's diet, including hidden sources (processed foods, shared cooking surfaces, condiments).
- Use of certified gluten-free complementary foods during the transition to solids.
- Awareness that a GFD can introduce nutritional gaps — particularly in B vitamins, iron, calcium, and fiber — and that early involvement of a registered dietitian is strongly recommended [Klemm2022].
- Anticipated clinical timeline: most infants show noticeable improvement within two weeks of strict GFD initiation [Schaer2024].

## 6. Contraindications and Adverse Effects

### Diagnostic Cautions
- **Do not test serologically before gluten introduction.** Pre-gluten serology is diagnostically unreliable and may produce falsely reassuring results [NICE2015].
- **Do not initiate GFD before diagnosis is confirmed.** Premature GFD normalizes antibody levels and mucosal histology, precluding accurate diagnosis [Klemm2022; NICE2015].
- **IgA deficiency** (occurring in approximately 2–3% of CD patients) produces false-negative TTG-IgA and EMA-IgA. Total IgA must be measured alongside TTG-IgA to identify this confounder [RubioTapia2023; Lemale2022].

### GFD-Related Adverse Effects
- **Nutritional inadequacy:** Gluten-free grain products are often lower in B vitamins, iron, fiber, and calcium. Infants on GFD require nutritional monitoring and dietary counseling [Klemm2022].
- **Accidental gluten exposure:** Even minimal exposure can perpetuate mucosal damage. Families require thorough education on hidden gluten sources [Klemm2022].

## 7. Special Populations

### Infants with a First-Degree Relative with CD
These infants carry an approximately 10% lifetime risk of developing CD (versus 1% in the general population) [Schaer2024]. HLA typing (DQ2/DQ8 genotyping) can rule out CD risk with near-certainty in HLA-negative infants, potentially eliminating the need for lifelong surveillance. For HLA-positive infants, regular serological screening after gluten introduction is warranted [Lopez2015].

### Infants with HLA-DQ2/DQ8 Positivity
Genetic predisposition without family history is also clinically relevant. Most carriers never develop CD, but they should be monitored following gluten introduction, particularly if symptomatic [Lopez2015; AndrenAronsson2023].

### Infants with Concurrent Type 1 Diabetes (T1D)
T1D and CD share HLA susceptibility alleles and frequently co-occur. Children with T1D have an approximately 5–10% prevalence of CD. ESPGHAN recommends specific management protocols for this population [Mearin2022].

### Infants with IgA Deficiency
Total IgA deficiency invalidates TTG-IgA and EMA-IgA testing. IgG-based antibody assays (TTG-IgG or DGP-IgG) should be used in this population [RubioTapia2023].

### Infants with Diagnostically Unclear Cases
When serological and clinical criteria do not align, or when TTG-IgA levels do not meet the nonbiopsy threshold, duodenal biopsy remains the standard for definitive diagnosis. ESPGHAN recommends individualized protocols for unclear cases [Mearin2022].

## 8. Monitoring Parameters

### During Diagnostic Workup
- Total serum IgA (to exclude IgA deficiency)
- TTG-IgA (first-line)
- EMA-IgA (confirmatory, particularly when nonbiopsy strategy is considered)
- Complete blood count (anemia screening)
- Iron studies, ferritin
- Consider duodenal biopsy if nonbiopsy criteria are not met [Lemale2022; RubioTapia2023]

### Following GFD Initiation
- **Antibody levels:** TTG-IgA should decline progressively with strict dietary adherence. Persistent elevation suggests ongoing gluten exposure [Mearin2022].
- **Growth parameters:** Weight and height should be plotted serially. Growth velocity is expected to normalize within months of effective GFD [NIDDK2024].
- **Mucosal healing:** Repeat duodenal biopsy may be indicated in children with persistent symptoms, slow antibody normalization, or complicated presentations (e.g., concurrent T1D) [Mearin2022].
- **Nutritional status:** Hemoglobin, iron, ferritin, folate, vitamin D, and calcium should be monitored periodically given the risk of GFD-related nutritional gaps [Klemm2022; Mearin2022].
- **Quality of life and adherence:** Structured family communication and age-appropriate patient education are recommended as integral components of follow-up care [Mearin2022].
