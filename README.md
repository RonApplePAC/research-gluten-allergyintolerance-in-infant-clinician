# Research Summary: Gluten Allergy/Intolerance in Infants

## Topic Overview

Gluten-related disorders in infancy represent a clinically significant and often underdiagnosed category of pediatric gastrointestinal disease. Celiac disease (CD) — an immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by gluten ingestion in genetically susceptible individuals — is the most consequential of these conditions, with a global prevalence estimated around 1 in 100. Despite its relative frequency, celiac disease in infants and young children remains underdiagnosed due to its heterogeneous clinical presentation, which extends well beyond the classic malabsorption syndrome to include subtle symptoms such as irritability, failure to thrive, anemia, and skin changes. The disease typically becomes clinically apparent after gluten-containing complementary foods are introduced, usually between 6 and 12 months of age.

A separate, non-immune-mediated condition known as non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) may also affect children. Unlike celiac disease, NCGS does not produce autoimmune damage to the small intestinal mucosa and lacks a validated serological or histological diagnostic marker. Diagnosis relies primarily on symptomatic response to gluten elimination followed by confirmed recurrence on rechallenge. Both conditions require careful clinical differentiation, as management, genetic counseling implications, and long-term sequelae differ substantially.

The evidence base around optimal gluten introduction timing in infancy has evolved considerably over the past decade. Large randomized controlled trials and systematic reviews have largely overturned earlier hypotheses that breastfeeding or delayed gluten introduction are protective against celiac disease development. Current guidelines from ESPGHAN (2016, updated 2024) and the ACG (2023) reflect this updated understanding, recommending gluten introduction between 4 and 12 completed months of age without restriction based on breastfeeding status, while noting that very high gluten quantities in early childhood may modestly elevate risk in genetically susceptible individuals.
